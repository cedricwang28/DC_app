Henry House

Thomas Henry

  • 1798: BORN

    • Thomas was born in Township of Drumbess, County Cavan, Ireland.

  • 1811: CANADA

    • Moved to Canada with his father John Henery and Nancy Bigger with their four sons and three daughters.

  • 1812: WAR TIME

    • Thomas was hired during the war as an attendant to judge Powell and travelled with the judge on several trips throughout Upper Canada. he also did some military duty until peace was restored.

  • 1815: FARM PURCHASE

    • John Henery moved the family to easy Whitby where he purchased a farm in what is now Lakeview Park, Oshawa.

    In the same year, Nancy Bigger Henry died and was one of the first burials in the Harbour Pioneer Cemetery.

    Thomas Henry married Elizabeth “Betsey” Davies (born October 1, 1800)

  • 1820: EMBARKED CAREER

    • Thomas Henry farmed his land and embarked upon a career as a public speaker.

  • 1829: LETTER OF SUPPORT

    • Thomas receives Christian Church gave Thomas letters of support.

    Thomas's wife died and was left alone to raise five sons.

  • 1830: Lurenda's Love

    • Met Lurenda Abbey of Hope Township and both got married.

  • 1832: Minister

    • Thomas became an ordained minister.

  • 1840: pastorate

    • Chosen as pastorate of the Church of Whitby which later became affiliated with the Christian Church.

  • 1843: First Church

    • The First Christian Church was built at the corner of Richmond and Church Streets.

  • 1850: Harbour Master

    • Thomas also became President of the Sydenham Harbour Company and became Harbour Master.

  • 1871: Trustee

    • Thomas was appointed Trustee of Starkey Seminary a college in the United States of the Christian denomination.

  • 1875: New Church

    • The new Christian Church was built on Centre Street, where Oshawa City Hall is located, and the church was named Centre Street United Church.

  • 1879: FINAL RESTING

    • Henry died at the age of 81.

  • 1912: William Henry

    • William Henry (Son of Thomas Henery) continued to farm until 1912.

  • New Landlords

    • The land was eventually purchased by R.S. McLaughlin and his brother George McLaughlin who turned it over to the City of Oshawa as a public park.

  • City of Oshawa

    • The stone house that Thomas lived in with his family still stands in Oshawa’s Lakeview Park. The house was built c. 1840, and Thomas lived in the home with Lurenda and a number of their children. Today the Henry family stone house is managed by The Oshawa Historical Society for the City of Oshawa as a museum.

    Guy House

    Thomas Guy Jr.

  • 1819: BORN

    • March 21, Thomas Guy Jr., older brother of James Odgers Guy of Guy House, Oshawa Community Museum, was born in St. Gorran, Cornwall, England.

  • 1842: MARRIED

    • Thomas Jr. married Harriet Cock.

  • 1843: FIRST DAUGHTER

    • First daughter Harriet was born.

  • 1844: SECOND DAUGHTER

    • Second daughter Ellen was born.

  • 1846: WELCOME TO CANADA

    • Thomas Jr. immigrated to Canada with his family, his mother-in-law Harriet Cock Sr., her male servant, and his wife.

  • 1847: THIRD CHILD

    • the family settled on the Reach Road where their third child William Billing Guy was born.

  • 1848: WIFE DIED

    • Thomas’ wife Harriet died of typhoid.

  • 1850: TRAVELLER

    • Thomas Jr. traveled to Woodstock with his brother James Odgers.

  • 1851: GUY'S POINT

    • Settling on Sydenham farm just west of Port Oshawa on Bonnie Brae Point (formerly Guy’s Point).

  • 1853: SECOND MARRIAGE

    • Thomas Jr. married Eliza Henry, the first child born to Lurenda and Elder Thomas Henry, and they had 5 children of their own, Eliza, Alford C., George, Frank T., and Emma.

  • 1867: SECOND WIFE DIED

    • Thomas’ second wife died of typhoid and is buried in the Harbour Cemetery.

    In the same year, Thomas Jr.’s first born daughter Harriet also died of typhoid in Bowmanville and is buried in the Harbour Cemetery.

  • 1869: THIRD MARRIAGE

    • Thomas Jr. remarried for the third time with Hannah Every. he had three more children, Thomas, Kirby and Petronella.

  • 1871: ACHIEVEMENT

    • One of the best farms in the County of Ontario: A Notice placed in the Ontario Reformer advertising for sale the Sydenham farm provides interesting details of the Guy farm.

    Sydenham farm compromised 200 acres.

    Thomas became a champion breeder and exhibitor of Ayrshire cattle, Shorthorn cattle, Leicester sheep and Berkshire pigs.

    Best known for his Ayrshires winning numerous prizes at the local, provincial and national levels.

  • 1879: THIRD WIFE DIED

    • Hannah died. Thomas Jr. remarried for the last time to Flora Douglas and they have one son, James Douglas Guy.

  • 1882: ACHIEVEMENT

    • The Farmer’s Advocate prize of $100.00 for best five cows at the Provincial Exhibition was won by five Ayrshires owned by Thomas Jr.

  • 1897: DIED

    • At the age of 79, Thomas Jr. Died. It appears Thomas Jr. never sold Sydenham farm. He was laid to rest in the Union Cemetery.

    Robinson House

    John Robinson

  • 1790: BORN

    • The Robinson Family can be traced back to Staindrope, County Durham, England with the birth of John Robinson.

  • 1812: MARRIAGE

    • John married Ann Boyns.

    This marriage gave John one daughter named Jane and a son named William who died at the age of 9 months.

    John and Ann’s daughter Jane married Joseph Vickers and had two daughters named Jane and Elizabeth.

  • 1816: SECOND HOPE

    • John's first wife died. He married Ruth Teneck afterwards.

    Their first years of marriage were spent in England, where they had eight children: William, Elizabeth, Ralph, Ann, John Ephriam, Mary, Benjamin, and Rachel.

  • 1833: VOYAGE TO CANADA

    • the family set out for a thirteen-week voyage to Canada. They then traveled by ox-cart and settled in Cobourg.

    John and Ruth stayed in Port Hope for six years and had two more children: Cornelius and Eunice.

  • 1839: HERE WE ARE OSHAWA!

    • Robinson’s moved to Oshawa, first settling on land now occupied by the Union Cemetery.

  • 1846: LAND OWNER

    • John Robinson purchased land from David Annis at Port Sydenham.

  • 1846 - 1851: YELLOW BRICK HOUSE

    • A yellow brick house was constructed on the property.

    John was a cobbler by trade in England and subsequently became a local entrepreneur, having a hand in many enterprises.

    It is said that he spent much of his time riding around the country.

  • THE U.S.A. IMMIGRANT

    • Eventually John immigrated to West Liberty, Iowa, the U.S.A. with two of his sons, possibly Ephriam and William.

    His wife Ruth chose to stay in Oshawa with the rest of the family.

  • 1861: BACK TO OSHAWA

    • John returned to Oshawa.

  • 1864: SECOND WIFE DIED

    • John's second wife Ruth died.

    John remarried while in West Liberty and fathered five more children with Mary Lawrence, one of whom died at a young age.

  • 1875: DEATH

    • John remained in West Liberty where he died and was buried. He does not have a headstone in the cemetery where he was buried.

  • LEGACY CONTINUES

    • The Robinson Family legacy continues in Oshawa not only with the remaining family, but also with the Robinson Family house. After their father’s death, John’s children conveyed the Robinson’s property to Cornelius Robinson by a deed dated August 2, 1875.

  • DIVISION

    • Cornelius then divided up the land, retaining the plot with the yellow brick house for himself.

  • 1922: CORNELIUS DIED

    • Cornelius died and his property was transferred to Eunice Ann Maynard, his daughter.

  • 1963: CITY OF OSHAWA TOOKOVER

    • After Mrs. Maynard’s death, the house was purchased by the City of Oshawa in an attempt to increase the City’s property around the lakefront for recreational purposes.

    After that time the house fell into disrepair.

  • TIME TO RESTORE THE HOUSE

    • The Oshawa and District Historical Society (now the Oshawa Historical Society) recognized the need to save and restore the house.

  • UNIQUE ARCHITECTURE

    • The historical significance of the house results not only from its link with the Robinson Family but also because of its unique architecture.

    The house has a gambrel roof constituting an essential part of the upper gallery, not often found in Canada. The Historical Society launched a campaign to restore the house as a centennial project.

  • 1969: FROM HOUSE TO MUSEUM

    • After restored, the house was opened as a museum.

    The house now stands as a lasting testament to one of Oshawa’s early families, the Robinsons.
